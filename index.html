How the count is made

Each field is corrected for uneven illumination (a percentile background estimated on a coarse grid, then divided out), thresholded by Otsu, opened to remove speckle, and hole-filled — live cells are refractile rings under bright field, and without hole filling each one segments as an annulus and counts twice. Connected components are measured, then a logistic-regression classifier rejects debris on six shape and intensity features. Touching cells are separated by a chamfer distance transform: every local maximum whose inscribed radius lies in a physical band around the expected cell radius counts as one cell. Cells touching the right or bottom border are dropped and those on the top or left are kept, so a cell straddling two fields is counted exactly once.

Why there is no neural network

A CNN trained on nothing is not a CNN, it is a confident random number generator — and this tool is aimed at places where nobody can cross-check its output against a haemocytometer and a trained eye. The classifier here is a logistic regression actually trained, in Node, on synthetic fields with exact ground truth, using the identical feature code the app runs. Its provenance is printed below. Once real annotated fields exist from actual use, a TF.js U-Net is a drop-in replacement for the segmentation stage — and the validation harness to prove it helps is already written.

Concentration

For a counted area A mm² over a chamber of depth d mm, concentration = N / (A·d) × 1000 × dilution cells/mL. With the standard Neubauer 1 mm² square at 0.1 mm depth this reduces to the familiar N × 10⁴ × dilution. The reported ± is the Poisson counting error 100/√N %, which is the irreducible uncertainty of counting N random events — count more fields to shrink it. If the field-to-field CV is much larger than that, the sample is not mixed.

Verification and limits

The engine is validated headlessly against synthetic fields with exact ground truth: mean absolute error 1.54 % over densities from 20 to 150 cells per field, viability within 1 %, and stable under 0.1–0.6 vignetting. Known limits: at debris loads above about half of all particles the count runs ~17 % high; cells cut by the top or left image border may be missed; out-of-focus fields flatten the refractile rim and depress the count; and the whole calibration collapses if the camera zoom changes after calibrating. All validation to date is synthetic — no real sample has been counted.

Research and education use only. Not a medical device and not a diagnostic. Counting cultured cells in a haemocytometer is what this is for. Do not use it to diagnose disease, count parasites, or make any clinical decision: that requires clinical validation, regulatory approval, and a trained microscopist — and getting it wrong in a place with no second opinion is how people get hurt.
