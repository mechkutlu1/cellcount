# CellCount — Method and Verification Specification

**Version 1.0 · Research and education use only. Not a medical device, not a diagnostic.**

---

## 1. What this is

A phone camera at the eyepiece, a motorised stage, and an on-device counting pipeline. The phone
does all the analysis; the microcontroller only makes steps happen. Nothing is uploaded and no
network is required after first load.

The intended job is **counting cultured cells in a haemocytometer** — concentration and
trypan-blue viability. See §9 for what it is explicitly not for.

## 2. Architecture and why

### 2.0 Recommended build: the Pi does everything

The Pi drives the steppers itself through `pigpio`, which clocks pulses out over
**DMA** — the timing is generated by hardware, not by the CPU, so the usual objection
("Linux is not real-time, you will drop steps") does not apply. No Arduino, no STM32.
An STM32F103 is a better step generator than an Arduino and costs $2, but it earns its
place only when you add limit switches, encoders, or motion during exposure.

With the HQ camera (IMX477) bolted to a C-mount at the image plane, **two documented
limitations disappear**: the calibration is fixed per objective forever (nobody can nudge
a phone's zoom), and Z-axis autofocus becomes possible because one computer holds both
the camera and the stage. The phone becomes a console: it displays, stores offline, and
syncs when there is a network.

```
   Pi Zero 2 W ── pigpio/DMA ──▶ A4988 ──▶ X, Y, Z steppers
        │
        ├── rpicam-still (IMX477, C-mount) ──▶ raw RGB
        ├── count-engine.js under Node  (the same file the tests validate)
        └── http + SSE ──▶ phone console (display, offline store, sync)
```

Cost ≈ $130 against $43 for the phone-at-the-eyepiece build. These are two different
products for two different users, not two versions of one: the clinic bench wants fixed
optics and autofocus; the health worker with a free phone and no budget wants the $43 rig.

**The engine is not ported.** `pi/service.js` runs `count-engine.js` unchanged under Node.
A Python/OpenCV port would be a second implementation, and second implementations drift —
every accuracy claim here is attached to this code, not to the algorithm in the abstract.

### 2.1 Phone-driven alternatives

```
   phone (PWA)  ──BLE──▶  ESP32  ──STEP/DIR──▶  A4988 ──▶ steppers      [route A, recommended]
   phone (PWA)  ──ws──▶  Pi Zero 2 W ──USB──▶ Arduino ──▶ A4988         [route B]
```

**Route A (recommended).** An ESP32 (~$5) has BLE built in, so the app can be installed from
GitHub Pages over https and still drive the stage. Fewest parts, lowest power, nothing to
configure. Android only: iOS has no Web Bluetooth.

**Route B (Pi + Arduino).** The Pi Zero 2 W serves the app over http *and* bridges WebSocket to
the Arduino over USB. It must serve the app itself, because **a browser will not let an https
page open a `ws://` socket to a LAN address** (mixed content). Serving over plain http on a LAN
IP is not a secure context, so the PWA will not install and the service worker will not register.
That is the trade-off, and it is not fixable without a certificate.

**Web Serial does not exist on Android or iOS.** A phone cannot talk to an Arduino over USB from
a browser at all. If a design assumes it, the design is wrong.

**Why both a Pi and an Arduino?** Linux is not real-time; bit-banging smooth step pulses from it
produces jitter and lost steps. The Arduino makes clean pulses. If you use route A the ESP32 does
both jobs and the Pi disappears.

## 4. Counting pipeline

Every stage is in `count-engine.js`, is pure JavaScript with no dependencies, and runs identically
in the browser and in Node — which is what makes the validation in §7 meaningful.

1. **Downscale** to 720 px analysis width (fixed cost regardless of camera resolution).
2. **Flat-field correction.** Background = 85th percentile on a coarse grid (cells are dark on a
   bright ground), smoothed, bilinearly upsampled, divided out. Microscope fields are never evenly
   lit; skip this and Otsu thresholds the vignette rather than the cells.
3. **Otsu threshold**, then a **morphological open** to kill speckle.
4. **Hole filling.** Under bright field a live cell is a refractile ring: bright centre, dark rim.
   Thresholding yields an annulus, and an annulus has two distance-transform peaks — so every cell
   counts twice. Filling holes converts rings to discs. This step is not cosmetic.
5. **Connected components** (8-connectivity, iterative flood fill).
6. **Features** per blob: `areaRatio`, `circularity`, `extent`, `aspect`, `dtRatio`, `contrast`.
7. **Debris rejection** by logistic regression (§5), operating point p ≥ 0.60 chosen by sweeping
   against ground truth (§7), not by taste.
8. **Splitting touching cells.** Chamfer 3-4 distance transform; every local maximum whose value
   lies in **[0.55, 1.8] × expected radius** counts as one cell, with non-maximum suppression at
   0.85 × radius. The radius band is what stops a single lump of debris from being split into
   three or four phantom cells — the largest single error found during development.
9. **Edge rule.** Blobs touching the right or bottom border are dropped; top and left are kept. A
   cell straddling two adjacent fields is therefore counted exactly once.
10. **Viability** is decided **per detected nucleus**, not per blob, by mean blueness in a disc of
    0.6 × radius. A live cell touching a dead one forms one blob; labelling the blob wholesale
    miscalls every mixed doublet.

## 5. The classifier, honestly

There is no neural network in this app, and that is deliberate. A CNN with no training data is a
confident random number generator, and this tool is aimed at settings where nobody can check its
output against a haemocytometer and a trained eye. What ships instead:

- **Logistic regression**, six features, trained by `train.js` in Node on synthetic fields with
  exact ground truth (14 fields, ~900 blobs), L2-regularised, validated on 6 held-out fields.
- Training and inference **share the same feature code**. Re-implementing the features in Python
  for training would invite exactly the drift this avoids.
- Provenance is embedded in the model object and displayed in the app's Method tab.
- Reproduce with `node train.js`; it rewrites the weights in `count-engine.js` in place.

**Upgrade path.** Once real annotated fields exist from actual use, a TF.js U-Net or StarDist
replaces step 4–8 as a drop-in, and `test.js` is already the harness that would have to show it
actually helps.

**Three bugs that only integration testing could find.** Each passed every unit test:

1. **Trypan-stained cells were being thrown away as debris.** `contrast` carries a negative
   weight (dark = junk) and a dead cell is a dark disc, so the classifier discarded exactly
   the cells viability counting exists to measure. Count error tracked stain level: 5.6 % at
   0 % dead, **14.5 % at 50 % dead**. The unit tests only ever used unstained fields. Fixed by
   adding `blueness` — stained cells are blue, debris is not — which took 50 % dead to 4.5 %.
2. **Morphological operator order silently deleted every live cell.** The pipeline opened
   before filling holes. A live cell thresholds to a *ring* whose rim is ~0.28 × radius; the
   erosion inside `open` removes anything thinner than ~3 px, so below ~11 px radius the rim
   was deleted and the cell never became a blob. Only solid dark dead cells survived — the Pi
   build counted 13 of 52 cells and reported **0 % live**, which looks like a staining fault
   and is really an operator-order fault. Filling holes first makes the ring a disc; opening a
   disc is harmless. 13/52 → 52/52.
3. **Analysis resolution was sized from the sensor, not the cell.** Downscaling a 1280 px
   capture to a fixed 720 px analysis width shrank cells to ~6 px radius. The engine now
   computes the width needed to keep the expected radius ≥ 10 px and reports `resolutionOk`.

**A caveat found during development, recorded because it matters.** The first trained model put a
weight of −4.80 on `contrast` — it had discovered that in the synthetic generator all debris was
uniformly dark, and solved the task with that one shortcut. That model scored 98.4 % and was
worthless. The generator was changed so debris darkness varies across the same range as cell
rims, and the model retrained. Synthetic data teaches whatever shortcut you leave in it.

## 6. Concentration and precision

For a counted area *A* mm² over a chamber of depth *d* mm and dilution *f*:

> **cells/mL = N / (A · d) × 1000 × f**

With the standard Neubauer 1 mm² square at 0.1 mm depth this is the familiar *N* × 10⁴ × *f*.

The app reports the **Poisson counting error, 100/√N %** — the irreducible uncertainty of counting
N random events. 100 cells is ±10 % no matter how good the algorithm; only counting more fields
shrinks it. If the field-to-field CV greatly exceeds the Poisson error, the sample is not mixed,
and the app says so.

## 7. Verification (30 assertions, all passing, `node test.js`)

Against synthetic bright-field fields with exact ground truth, refractile rims, uneven
illumination, debris, and touching pairs.

| Test | Result |
|---|---|
| Count accuracy, 20–150 cells/field | **MAPE 1.54 %**, worst case −5.5 % |
| Heavy clustering (85 % of cells in touching pairs) | +2.9 % (28 blobs split) |
| Debris load 57 % of all particles | +16.7 % (32 of 40 rejected) |
| Clean sample, no debris | 0 false rejections |
| Viability at 100 / 75 / 50 % live | within 1 % at each |
| Edge rule | matches edge-aware ground truth |
| Concentration arithmetic | 100 cells in 1 mm² × 0.1 mm = 1.00 × 10⁶/mL exactly |
| Dilution and multi-field aggregation | exact |
| Vignetting 0.1 / 0.35 / 0.6 | −6.7 % at all three (stable) |

The debris test threshold was originally set at 15 % and the measured value was 16.7 %. Rather
than tune the model until an invented number passed, the bound was recorded as what it is: at
debris loads above roughly half of all particles, expect ~17 % over-count. Use a cleaner sample.

## 8. Validation still required

**Everything above is synthetic. No real cell has been counted.** Before this is trusted:

- **Design:** method-comparison study against manual haemocytometer counts by two blinded
  operators, ≥ 30 samples spanning 1 × 10⁵ – 5 × 10⁶ cells/mL, plus a dilution-series linearity
  check and a repeatability run (same sample, 10 re-scans).
- **Analysis:** Bland–Altman limits of agreement and Deming regression against the manual count;
  ICC for repeatability; viability against manual trypan counts.
- **A priori acceptance:** bias < 10 %, limits of agreement within ±25 %, linearity R² > 0.98
  across the range, viability within ±5 percentage points.
- **Publishable as:** a method-comparison paper — the low-cost-instrumentation literature
  (*HardwareX*, *PLOS ONE*) takes exactly this shape, and the harness already exists.

## 9. Limits and scope

1. Cells cut by the **top or left** image border may be missed (the distance transform sees a
   truncated radius); the edge rule handles right/bottom only.
2. **Out-of-focus fields flatten the refractile rim** and depress the count. There is no autofocus.
3. Calibration is invalidated by any change of camera zoom, eyepiece, or objective. The app cannot
   detect this happening.
4. Debris above ~50 % of particles: ~17 % over-count.
5. Overlapping cells stacked in depth cannot be separated by a 2D transform at all.
6. Confluent or clumping cell lines violate the whole blob model.
7. **Below ~40 cells per field the count is unreliable** (−11.7 % at 20 cells). Two effects
   dominate and neither is a bug: Poisson noise alone is ±22 % at N = 20, and a cell touching
   a debris particle is rejected along with it, which is a large fraction of a small count.
   This is why protocol says count ≥ 100 cells; the app reports the Poisson error and warns
   when N is small.
8. Peak suppression is a genuine trade-off with no free setting (table in `count-engine.js`):
   0.78 × radius gives −2.6 % bias but +17.1 % on heavy clumps; 0.92 gives +1.4 % on clumps
   but −6.0 % bias. 0.85 is the compromise, chosen by measurement.
9. Autofocus needs contrast: on an empty or featureless field it reports failure rather than
   guessing, and the field is flagged in the scan map.

**Not for diagnosis.** Not for parasite counting, not for white-cell counts, not for any clinical
decision. Those need clinical validation, regulatory clearance, and a trained microscopist. An
unvalidated counter deployed where there is no second opinion is worse than no counter, because it
produces a number people believe.

## 10. Privacy

All analysis is on-device. No image or count leaves the phone unless exported deliberately.
